Gramática — Grammar Notes

Week 6 · Two key structures: possessives and saber vs. conocer

G1

Adjetivos Posesivos · Possessive Adjectives

mi · tu · su · nuestro — agree with the noun's number (and gender for nuestro)

Possessive adjectives in Spanish agree with the thing possessed, not with the owner. They come before the noun and match it in number — and for nuestro, also in gender.

Singular Plural Examples
mi (my) mis (my) mi clase / mis clases
tu (your, informal) tus (your) tu libro / tus libros
su (his/her/your-formal) sus (his/her/your) su familia / sus hermanos
nuestro/a (our) nuestros/as (our) nuestro profesor / nuestras notas

Key pattern: agreement is with the noun, not the speaker. Mis hermanos = "my brothers" (plural noun → plural possessive), regardless of how many speakers.

⚠ Pitfall · Cuidado

mi and tu as possessives carry no accent mark.

The accented forms mí (me) and tú (you) are pronouns — different word, different role.

mi familia = my family · para mí = for me

tu hermano = your brother · tú eres simpático = you are nice

Examples · Ejemplos

Mi madre se llama Ana. → My mother's name is Ana.

Sus abuelos viven en Quito. → His/her/their grandparents live in Quito.

Nuestra clase de español es difícil. → Our Spanish class is hard.

¿Dónde están tus primos? → Where are your cousins?

G2

Saber vs. Conocer · Two Verbs for "To Know"

SABER — facts, information, how to · CONOCER — people, places, familiarity

Spanish has two verbs that both translate as "to know" in English. Choosing between them depends on what kind of knowledge you have. Both are irregular only in the yo form.

Pronoun SABER facts / how to CONOCER people / places
yo sé (irregular) conozco (irregular)
tú sabes conoces
él/ella/Ud. sabe conoce
nosotros sabemos conocemos
ellos/Uds. saben conocen

When to use SABER · Cuándo usar SABER

SABER + infinitivo = to know how to (do something)

¿Sabes cocinar? → Do you know how to cook?

SABER + información / hecho = to know a fact

Sé dónde está la biblioteca. → I know where the library is.

When to use CONOCER · Cuándo usar CONOCER

CONOCER + persona = to know (be acquainted with) a person

Conozco a tu profesor. → I know your professor.

CONOCER + lugar = to be familiar with a place

¿Conoces Buenos Aires? → Are you familiar with Buenos Aires?

⚠ The Personal "a" · La a personal

When conocer takes a person as its direct object, you must add the personal a right before the person.

✓ Conozco a María. — I know María.

✗ Conozco María. (incorrect)

No personal a with places: Conozco Bogotá. ✓

Quick Decision · ¿Saber o Conocer?

A fact? → saber  ·  How to do something? → saber

A person? → conocer + a  ·  A place? → conocer

Cultura — Family & Languages in the Spanish-Speaking World

How family structures and multilingualism shape daily life across Spain and Latin America

👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 La Familia · Family Structures & Roles

🏠

Multi-generational households

In many Spanish-speaking countries it's still common for adult children to live with their parents until marriage — and for grandparents (los abuelos) to live in the same home or nearby. This isn't seen as unusual or as a sign of dependence — it's a default, often economically practical and culturally valued.

🎉

Sunday family lunch

The big almuerzo dominical (Sunday lunch) is a cornerstone tradition across Latin America and Spain. Cousins (primos), aunts and uncles (tíos/tías) and grandparents gather weekly. Missing it without good reason is sometimes treated as a small social transgression.

👪

Compound family terms

Spanish uses the masculine plural to cover mixed groups: los padres = parents (mom + dad), los hermanos = siblings (brothers + sisters), los tíos = aunts & uncles, los abuelos = grandparents. There's no separate word for "siblings" — context disambiguates.

💍

Two surnames

In most Spanish-speaking countries, people carry two surnames: father's first, then mother's. Gabriel García Márquez = García (father) + Márquez (mother). Women don't typically take a husband's name on marriage — your full name stays the same throughout life.

🗺 Lenguas · The Linguistic Landscape

🇪🇸

Spain: four co-official languages

Spanish (castellano) is the national language, but Catalan (català), Galician (galego), and Basque (euskera) are co-official in their respective regions. A child in Barcelona grows up bilingual in Catalan and Spanish from birth — both are everyday languages, not "second languages."

🇵🇪

Indigenous languages still alive

Latin America is home to hundreds of indigenous languages still in daily use. Quechua (Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador) has ~8 million speakers; Guaraní is co-official with Spanish in Paraguay; Náhuatl is spoken across central Mexico by ~1.5 million people.

🌎

The Spanish you'll hear varies

Spanish has notable regional flavors. Argentinian Spanish replaces tú with vos (vos sos instead of tú eres). Caribbean Spanish drops final s sounds. Spaniards use vosotros for "you all" while Latin Americans use ustedes. All mutually intelligible — like American/British/Australian English.

🎵

Multilingual artists

Many Latin artists move fluidly between languages. Shakira sings in Spanish, English, Portuguese, and Arabic. Bad Bunny code-switches Spanish/English in the same line. Multilingualism is normal in Spanish-speaking pop culture, not a special skill.
